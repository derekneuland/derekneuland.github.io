The Perfect Storm for a Creative Renaissance

May 18, 2022

Reply Forward

We are on the verge of a creative renaissance. The world is changing and technology is evolving at an unprecedented rate. This is opening up new opportunities for creativity and expression.

It has never been easier to be a creator. With the tools and resources available today, anyone can start creating and sharing their work with the world. This is driving a surge in creativity and innovation. Going one step further, advancements in natural language processing technology has brought on a new world to create inside of. Thanks to machine learning models such as OpenAI's GPT-3, it is now possible for any text you can imagine to be generated by a machine. Dalle-2 is doing the same for still images. In the next 3-5 years, I imagine we will be able to input a movie script and the output will be a movie that could premier in theaters around the world (I love the idea of fan fiction versions of movies. The ability to take fan created versions of existing characters and universes and see them realized visually will be really exciting to see).

That being said, I don't think these tools will replace the creativity of humans. I believe these innovations will help us become more creative by alleviating the parts of creativity we don't enjoy. Think about your personal creation process and every step you take from idea to publish. What are your favorite steps? What are your least favorite steps? Finding tools that can help automate your least favorite steps could free your time and energy up so you can focus on creating more impactful and meaningful art.

We are seeing new forms of expression emerge, through mediums like virtual reality and augmented reality. These technologies are giving people new ways to experience and interact with the world around them.

We are also seeing a rise in popularity of DIY and maker culture. People are taking matters into their own hands and creating things that they want to see in the world. This trend is only going to continue as we see more people empowered to create their own products and experiences.

All of these factors are coming together to create a perfect storm for a creative renaissance. We are on the brink of something big, something special. So let's embrace it, nurture it, and see where it takes us.


Redefining the Term “Lifetime”

April 18,2022

Reply Forward

I think we need to re-examine what a “lifetime” should be defined as. Unlike how “literally” became redefined out of ignorance, I believe rethinking the definition of “lifetime” is one of adapting to an ever changing and growing world.

The idea of “lifetime” has been engrained in our vocabulary for decades. Lifetime warranty. Lifetime guarantee. “That will last you a lifetime!” The idea was it would last you your entire life. Until you could no longer physically use it.

This also translates to careers and relationships. When have you heard the phrase “that job will last you until you're retired/dead” lately? I haven't. “Till death do us part” is a disgusting old patriarchal saying that guilts people into staying in unhappy and/or abusive relationships and marriages.

I believe a lifetime should be defined how you want to define it.

People have called them “phases” in the past. But that diminishes them. You could call it a “period of my life”, but that also feels like it was just temporary. As if it was a rest stop on your way to your “finish line”.

I think it's important we acknowledge and appreciate what we can from our past lifetimes. They weren't perfect. We made some really dumb mistakes. But we grew from them. We learned about ourselves. We learned about the world. We learned how to exist.

I am extremely guilty of diminishing my self worth. I am really harsh on myself when I think about my past. Mistakes I made. Poor choices. Uninformed decisions. Lack of focus.

Then I remind myself: I was in my 20's.

I was still a child. I was still learning how to exist. I didn't know I had ADHD or anxiety or severe depression. I didn't know I was non-binary or queer yet. I didn't know who I was yet. Truthfully, I still don't. But i'm learning more everyday.


Why Gender Neutral Language Matters

March 16, 2022

Reply Forward

The following collection of thoughts and ideas assumes you believe there are more genders than the binary male and female, what non-binary gender is, what gender neutral pronouns are, and most importantly you want to learn how you can be more inclusive. Oh, also that you're familiar with The Office.

Giving your website a more gender-neutral tone can be extremely helpful in making everyone feel welcome. In the United States, there are over 1.2 million adults who are non-binary. Even beyond that staggering amount (which is 11% of LGBTQ+ adults in the US), an increasing number of humans are using gender pronouns other than he/him and she/her, and not all identify as transgender or non-binary. Gender neutral language is becoming more prevalent and accepted every year.

In the English language, "he" has often been used as an automatic fill-in for generic individuals. By using gender-neutral language, you're ensuring your sentences are inclusive for everyone. For example, if you are describing someone performing an action or behavior, unless the gender of the individual is important to the sentence, gender neutral pronouns can be used to ensure those reading your website feel included.

e.g. This candle is so decadent and fragrant you won't want to wait until he comes over to light it.

This description for a candle (that I wrote for this example) makes it sound like the only people this candle is for is people who date men or male identifying individuals. If it was worded “you won't want to wait until they come over to light it.”, it doesn't lose any meaning and it's more inclusive.

Using gender neutral language helps to break down the harmful binary system which can be limiting for everyone. The pronouns "he and she" are two extreme binaries that don't leave room for other gender identities. This can be limiting or harmful for individuals who don't identify with either pronoun.

There have been many studies about the positive effect of using gender neutral pronouns. Making a conscious effort to use more inclusive language on your website will help millions of people feel more included, as well as avoid falling into any unintentional gender stereotypes (Michael Scott's well timed but cringeworthy “that's what she said” tagline comes to mind).
